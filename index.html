Analysis of the Fire and Emergency Medical Incidents in Kent, WA

Author: Justin Callejo

February 4, 2025

The maps from Figures 1-5 represent the fire and emergency medical incidents that were called in at Kent, WA in 2016. In order for these maps to be produced and perform spatial analyses properly, parameters and limits were set. First the workspace was set to the Kent Fire Department’s Risk geodatabase file and transformed to WGS_1984_(ITRF00)_To_Nad_1983 to ensure a universal transformation of all the data that is being worked with. Then the extent was set to the Kent Fire Department. Our last setup step was to determine the Raster Analysis cell size to 50 which is the typical width of a typical four-lane street.

The method used to display the different types of incidents in Figures 1-5 was the use of the Kernel Density Tool. This uses a grid based on a given search radius (in this case, 5280), area unit (sq. miles), and output cell size (50 to match raster environment). This step was repeated for every incident as noted in Figures 1-5 respectively to showcase the different “hotspots” of each incident type.

To further define the spatial hotspots of these incidents, the Contour with Barriers tool was utilized to manually create discrete contour intervals. Each type of incident had its own appropriate contour intervals and were set using this tool.

At this point there are 5 density raster events with their corresponding density contour events all in separate layer files. To clean things up, Group layers for both the density rasters and density contours were imported and linked to these separate layer files.

Figure 1. Dot Density and Kernel Density Map with Contour Intervals of All Emergency Response Incidents Called into the Kent Fire Department in 2016. Synthetic subset data provided by Kent Fire Department.

Looking at all Emergency Responses in Figure 1, the urban parts of Kent (near shoWare center, Kent Station, WA167) seem to have the highest concentration of incidents. In particular, just above the intersection of WA167 and SR516 is a hotspot of an average 500 incidents per sq. mile in that area. It is noteworthy that there are a handful of grocery stores and restaurants within that area.

Figure 2. Kernel Density Map with Contour Intervals of All Structure Fire Incidents Called into the Kent Fire Department in 2016. Synthetic subset data provided by Kent Fire Department.

Narrowing down to only Structure Fire Incidents in Figure 2, the same concentration is found at the intersection mentioned in Figure 1’s discussion. However, a larger, secondary concentration can be found at the Southwestern border of the city. A closer inspection of the area reveals many Automotive service shops and coupled with grocery stores/markets in the area.

Figure 3. Kernel Density Map with Contour Intervals of All Rescue/Emergency Medical Service Incidents Called into the Kent Fire Department in 2016. Synthetic subset data provided by Kent Fire Department.

In Figure 3, the data shows that Rescue and Emergency Medical Service Incidents are concentrated around that same intersection but seems to extend eastward where WA515 begins: East Hill. This could possibly be explained by the fact that there is a Senior Living Center there.

Figure 4. Kernel Density Map with Contour Intervals of All Service Call Incidents Called into the Kent Fire Department in 2016. Synthetic subset data provided by Kent Fire Department.

In Figure 4, there are two high concentrations of Service Call incidents, with the unique one being just east of the WA515 and SR516 intersection. There are a handful of townhomes within this concentration which could explain the surge in service calls in the area.

Figure 5. Kernel Density Map with Contour Intervals of All Fire, Explosion, and Hazardous Materials Incidents Called into the Kent Fire Department in 2016. Synthetic subset data provided by Kent Fire Department.

In Figure 5, The same highest contour interval concentration is present. However there seems to be many different second-highest contour interval (10 per sq mi) concentrations. These same concentrations have been found in previous figures which could be partially explained by a specific grocery store–Fred Meyer– which almost always has its own gas station. Additionally, the southeastern-most concentration is around Lake Meridian Park where people could be starting bonfires that could have gone out of control.

        Kernel Density Maps are effective because they can portray highly concentrated areas of certain incidents while keeping individual addresses and information concealed through Kernel Density’s nature to generalize its data (heat blobs of data rather than points). Furthermore, Contour Intervals outline these concentrations at various levels to further clarify and highlight these areas.
